## Why this page works

NotebookLM makes an unfamiliar product legible through a consistent proof sequence:

```text
Specific outcome
  -> visible result
  -> source-to-result mechanism
  -> breadth of useful formats
  -> concrete examples
  -> testimonial / trust
  -> privacy assurance
  -> questions before commitment
```

The rhythm is effective because each section answers the next practical professional question: *What do I get? How does it work? What else can it make? Has it worked for work like mine? Can I trust it?*

## What WorkshopLM should borrow

| NotebookLM pattern | Professional WorkshopLM translation |
| --- | --- |
| A single output-led hero | Lead with a finished, editable professional deliverable: a client-ready deck, briefing, or video—not a generic AI workspace. |
| Feature-specific hero variants | Frame each demo/story around a work outcome: turn a client workshop into a decision deck; turn research into an approved brief; turn a brief into a reviewable video. |
| Source convergence diagram | Show the actual professional chain: meeting, files, and research → grounded Map → approved Brief + Style → deck, storyboard, and video. Put provenance and sign-offs in the diagram. |
| Output carousel | Use a compact horizontal `Production` rail: Presentation, Brief, Infographic, Storyboard, Video. Give each artifact its status, source coverage, and version—not a decorative illustration. |
| Example notebook cards | Use `Workshop examples` with credible work contexts: board decision, client proposal, leadership offsite, and research synthesis. Cards should include the input mix, resulting artifact, and a proof cue such as `12 cited claims` or `Brief approved`. |
| Privacy section | Make a concrete local-demo claim only where evidence supports it: local sources stay in the configured local data root; generated artifacts and provenance are inspectable. Do not make broad training, retention, or enterprise-security promises without current policy evidence. |
| FAQ close | Answer adoption objections: What can I bring in? What is source-grounded? Can I edit and export it? What changes after a source changes? What is local versus provider-backed? |

## What to change for the WorkshopLM brand

### Positioning

NotebookLM is education- and discovery-oriented: assignments, summaries, learning formats, and broad exploration. WorkshopLM should lead with accountable work product:

> **Turn raw working sessions into finished work you can defend.**

Supporting promise:

> Bring meetings, research, and working files into one grounded Workshop. Shape the evidence into an approved brief, then produce editable, source-traceable deliverables.

The public page should make three differentiated promises visible early:

1. **Grounded:** claims trace to an exact source excerpt.
2. **Governed:** Map, Brief, Style, and Storyboard use explicit sign-offs and honest `Needs update` states.
3. **Finished:** the system produces editable professional outputs, not only answers or summaries.

### Visual language

- Keep the long-scroll editorial pacing, generous whitespace, and one idea per section.
- Use WorkshopLM’s restrained neutral system with a decisive, accessible accent taken from the selected brand style—not NotebookLM’s soft blue illustration language.
- Prefer real product captures, cropped artifact previews, source-trace callouts, and tasteful CSS composition over generic AI art or school-like iconography.
- Use editorial typography, sharp hierarchy, rules/dividers, and dense metadata. Reserve rounded cards for actual deliverables and selected workshop examples.
- Make motion serve evidence: a source excerpt resolving to a Map claim, then to a deck block. Do not make it decorative.

### Language to avoid

Avoid `assignments`, `study`, `learning`, `flashcards`, `quiz`, `brainstorm better`, and claims that WorkshopLM is a generic design suite or universally secure enterprise software. Prefer `workshop`, `source scope`, `decision`, `brief`, `review`, `version`, `source trace`, `client-ready`, and `professional output`.

## Recommended page architecture

```text
Header
  Product / How it works / Example workshops / Proof
  [Watch the 3-minute demo]

1. Hero — finished-work promise
  "Turn raw thinking into finished work."
  Product capture: a real deck with a source-trace inspector open
  Primary CTA: Watch the demo
  Secondary CTA: See the workflow

2. The professional gap
  Raw meeting notes and research become untraceable as they move into decks,
  briefs, and videos. WorkshopLM keeps the thread intact.

3. How WorkshopLM works — proof diagram
  Sources -> Grounded Map -> Approved Brief + Style -> Editable Outputs -> Approved Storyboard -> Video
  Under each step: a concrete proof cue (citation, sign-off, version, or source count).

4. Three production controls
  Source-grounded claims / Approval gates / Version-aware outputs
  Small real product moments, not feature-card wallpaper.

5. Outputs that stay connected to the work
  Presentation (hero) / Brief / Infographic / Storyboard / Video
  Each card shows status, version, and proof metadata.

6. Example workshops
  Board decision / Client proposal / Leadership workshop / Research synthesis
  Each card connects an input set to one finished deliverable.

7. Trust and evidence boundary
  Local-first demo path, source visibility, explicit provider-backed labels,
  and no unsupported privacy claim.

8. FAQ
  Clear answers to workflow, editing, source, versioning, and demo questions.

9. Final CTA
  Watch how the Build Week demo itself was made in WorkshopLM.
```

## Ship-order recommendation

For Build Week, do not build a broad marketing site before the demo proof exists. The right first public surface is a concise, evidence-led landing page built from current verified artifacts:

1. one finished Presentation or video as the hero proof;
2. the exact workflow diagram above;
3. one trustworthy source-trace interaction or still;
4. current, qualified privacy/setup copy;
5. the under-three-minute demo as the primary CTA.

Add testimonials, feature carousels, or polished example galleries only when they are backed by real product outputs. This preserves NotebookLM’s strong information architecture while making WorkshopLM read as a serious professional production system.
